New River Train

Photo by Colin Lloyd on Unsplash

This is an old song that is thought to refer to the New River Line train that ran in the New River area of southwestern Virginia starting in the late 19th century. There’s actually a historical marker in the town of Fries, Virginia dedicated to this song; per the marker’s inscription, the earliest recording of this song was by Henry Whitter in 1924. This song would go on to become popular in both old-time and then bluegrass circles, and notable recordings by old-time/bluegrass artists include the Monroe brothers in 1936, and Tony Rice and Norman Blake in 1987.

The chord progression is straightforward, with no unusual chords or quirks. The verse and chorus have the same melody, and the lyrics are fairly simple; the verses aren’t quite as repetitive as the songs that repeat the same line three times and then tack on the same closing line each time, but this song’s verses are pretty repetitive nonetheless, mostly harping on how the singer’s darling fails at loving people… which seems to be a rather counterproductive thing to be lecturing one’s sweetheart about if the singer actually wishes to keep said sweetheart! But given that the singer keeps repeating in the chorus that the New River Train is going to be taking them away again, perhaps the singer is trying to shoo their darling away preemptively….

Regardless, the simple nature of the melody and the lyrics make this a good song for beginning jammers, and the harmony parts are straightforward too.
