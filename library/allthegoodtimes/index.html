All the Good Times are Past and Gone

Photo by Jan Kopřiva on Unsplash

This is a waltz that has been recorded many times over; perhaps the earliest recording was by Fred and Gertrude Gosset in 1930. The song as they recorded is very similar to more modern versions, although they take it at a pretty dolefully slow tempo, and the last line of the chorus as they sing it, “darlin’, don’t weep for me” is somewhat different from how many people sing it nowadays, usually either “little darlin’, don’t weep no more,” or “little darlin’, don’t you weep no more”, that last version being the version that I usually sing.

Similar to many older songs in the blues and old-time music traditions, this song features what are known as ‘floating’ verses or floating lyrics, which are lyrics that are used in many different songs, thereby ‘floating’ from song to song. One funny thing about the most common set of lyrics for the song as it is sung today is that the verses are fairly contradictory regarding the singer’s feelings about the ’little darling’ being sung to. In the first verse, the singer wishes they’d never met their little darling, and by the last verse the singer is asking their darling to come back and calling them their one true love… the singer is clearly a person who can’t make up their mind!
