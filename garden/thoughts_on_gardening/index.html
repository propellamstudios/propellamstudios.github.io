On keeping a thought garden

Thoughts on cultivating a thought garden

Like many other people, I’ve thought about creating a blog before, but the thought of having to publish fully formed, polished essays at a regular cadence felt quite intimidating. And so after I learned about the idea of digital gardens, where creators can jot down notes that interlink to each other, in various stages of growth and refinement – that idea held a lot of appeal. This is my attempt at creating my own digital garden, and it feels fitting that this garden is going to be centered on the world of folk and traditional music, since this is similarly a world where the flowering of creativity is nonlinear, and musicians from different genres and traditions borrow from each other and inspire each other. I hope that others will find some inspiration in the garden that I’m cultivating here.

I do find it a bit ironic that after looking through a bunch of different blogs and considering different ways to record my thoughts, I ended up picking a method that is decidedly nonlinear. The reason why I find that ironic is I tend to work in a very linear fashion - or at least, when I’m actually at my most productive, it’s generally because I’ve gotten myself to work in a linear fashion and have just been able to proceed down a checklist of items. There’s a real tangible satisfaction at being able to check off a box and to know that a thing is completed; but I think that the reason why that style of work doesn’t quite fit with my own sensibility of the type of written record that I’d like to produce is that I think the pressure of producing completely polished, finished pieces of work on a regular basis feels quite intimidating. Of course, this pressure is completely self-inflicted, to be sure - I know a lot of people do treat their blogs as scratch notebooks, where even unfinished thoughts get jotted down. But there’s something very appealing about being able to revisit the same subject and add more thoughts, for new ideas to flower and for other ideas to be pruned back.
