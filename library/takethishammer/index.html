Take this Hammer

Photo by iMattSmart on Unsplash

This is a hammer song, in the tradition of other hammer songs like “Nine Pound Hammer,” and “Spike Driver Blues”, and this subgenre of songs is closely related to - or perhaps the same - as the subgenre of songs about the mythical figure of John Henry. These ‘hammer’ or ‘roll’ songs are thought to have originated as work songs, sung by forced labor convict gangs or other work crews laboring on railroads or doing logging. Many of these songs share ‘floating’ verses, i.e. verses that are shared between different songs, blurring the line that differentiates these songs from each other.

A very early recording of this song is a field recording taken by John and Ruby Lomax in 1939 at Raiford Penitentiary in Florida; the earliest commercial recording appears to by by Lead Belly, released in 1942. Both of these early recordings include a rhythmic ‘ha!’ at the end of each line, which per Lead Belly was when the hammer would fall as the song was sung by a work gang. Later versions by bluegrass artists removed this phrasing; recordings include a live version by Flatt and Scruggs in 1963, and an album version by the Osborne Brothers, also in 1963.

This is an easy song to lead at a jam as the lyrics to this song are repetitive, and the melody for the verse and chorus are the same. Various recordings take different tempos, so you can pick a speed that you’re comfortable with and forge ahead with it.
